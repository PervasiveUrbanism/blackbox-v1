# How Do We Preserve What We Learn?

## AI, Organisational Memory and Architectural Practice

*A case study of BlackBox V1*

**Start reading:** https://pervasiveurbanism.github.io/blackbox-v1/publication/00-overview.html

---

This publication documents *BlackBox V1*, an experimental AI workspace developed between February and May 2026.

The project explored how artificial intelligence might support the knowledge, culture, and everyday workflows of an architectural practice.

At first glance, BlackBox appears to be a technical project involving Large Language Models, image archives, documentation systems, and open-source software. However, the central question was less about AI itself and more about organisational memory:

**How do we preserve what we learn?**

Architectural practices continuously generate knowledge through projects, presentations, visual references, technical solutions, workflows, and design discussions. Over time, these outputs form a collective memory that reflects the accumulated experience of the organisation.

The central hypothesis behind BlackBox was that this organisational memory could influence AI systems without requiring specialised model training.

Rather than attempting to create a bespoke architectural AI, the project explored whether a general-purpose model could become more useful by gaining access to the knowledge already present within a practice. In much the same way that architects learn through exposure to projects, references, and conversations, the system was designed to engage with the accumulated output of the organisation itself.

The objective was therefore not to create an AI that *became* the office. The objective was to create an environment in which AI conversations could become informed by the knowledge, references, and experience of a particular practice.

To investigate this idea, BlackBox combined:

- local and cloud-based Large Language Models;
- conversational AI interfaces;
- visual reference archives;
- office documentation systems;
- collaborative environments;
- AI-assisted software development.

The publication documents:

- the motivation behind the project;
- the technical prototype;
- the development process;
- observations and lessons learned;
- broader reflections on AI and organisational memory.

## A Note on the Writing Process

There is a certain irony in a publication about AI-assisted knowledge systems being written with the assistance of AI.

The text presented here was developed through an extended dialogue with ChatGPT. The structure, drafts, revisions, editorial discussions, and many of the diagrams emerged through this collaborative process. Every chapter was reviewed, rewritten, expanded, challenged, and refined through repeated iterations.

At the same time, the publication was not generated automatically. The arguments, examples, observations, images, diagrams, and final editorial decisions remained under human direction throughout.

In this sense, the publication became a small experiment in its own right.

Not a single sentence was written without AI assistance, yet the resulting text reflects a specific project, a particular experience, and a series of decisions that could only have emerged through the interaction between human judgement and machine support.
