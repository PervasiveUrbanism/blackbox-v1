# Conclusion

## From archive to conversation

BlackBox began with a question that is familiar to many architectural practices:

**How do we preserve what we learn?**

The question is not only administrative. Architectural projects generate knowledge continuously, but much of that knowledge becomes difficult to access once a project ends. Documents are archived, images are stored in libraries, workflows evolve, prompts are forgotten, and experience disperses across teams and individuals. The result is not an absence of knowledge, but a loss of connection to knowledge already produced.

Artificial intelligence offered a new way of approaching this problem.

Rather than treating archives as static repositories, AI introduced the possibility of interacting with accumulated knowledge through conversation. Images could become searchable through language. Documentation could become easier to navigate. Information distributed across different systems could become accessible through a common interface. Previous projects, technical guidance, visual references, and internal workflows could begin to participate more actively in current work.

BlackBox explored this possibility through a practical prototype.

The resulting system was modest in scale, but deliberately broad in scope. It combined open-source tools, local language models, documentation platforms, image archives, collaborative environments, and AI-assisted development workflows into a working ecosystem. Some components proved immediately useful. Others remained experimental. Several suggested further directions for research.

The significance of the project therefore does not lie in the software alone. The prototype was the means through which a wider set of questions could be tested.

The first concerned organisational memory. BlackBox suggested that AI can help practices retrieve and reuse knowledge that already exists, rather than only generate new content. The value lies not simply in storing information, but in making it accessible at the moment when it can inform design, coordination, research, or decision-making.

The second concerned context. A general-purpose AI system becomes more useful when it can operate within the specific knowledge environment of a practice. It does not necessarily need to become office-specific through training. It can become more relevant through structured access to documents, images, references, workflows, and terminology that already define the practice.

The third concerned agency. AI-assisted development lowers the threshold for creating bespoke digital tools. Problems that were previously too specific or too small to justify conventional software development can now become subjects of experimentation, provided that implementation is accompanied by testing, documentation, maintenance, and critical judgement.

Together, these observations suggest a different way of thinking about AI in architectural practice. Its value may not be limited to producing images, text, or design proposals. It may also lie in helping practices organise what they know, situate general intelligence within local contexts, and develop tools around their own methods of working.

Many of the technologies used within BlackBox will change. Models will improve, interfaces will evolve, and some of the applications described in this publication may eventually be replaced. The technical configuration of the prototype should therefore not be understood as a fixed model to be reproduced.

The underlying questions are more durable.

Architectural practices will continue to generate drawings, reports, images, workflows, conversations, and ideas that represent years of accumulated experience. They will continue to face the difficulty of making that experience available to future projects and future colleagues. They will also continue to encounter small, specific problems that generic software does not address.

BlackBox does not propose a definitive model for the future architectural office. It documents one experiment, carried out at a particular moment in the development of AI, and the observations that emerged from it.

Its conclusion is therefore deliberately limited.

AI will not resolve the complexity of architectural practice. It will not replace the judgement through which knowledge becomes meaningful. It may, however, provide new ways of connecting the fragments of knowledge that practices already produce and allowing them to remain active within future work.

In that sense, organisational memory can become something more than an archive.

It can become part of an ongoing conversation.
