# Why I Started BlackBox

## From curiosity about open-source AI to an architectural experiment

**BlackBox** did not begin with a detailed proposal, a technical specification, or a clearly defined development roadmap.

It began with a practical curiosity about artificial intelligence and a desire to understand what this rapidly developing technology might mean for architectural practice. At first, the interest was not directed towards a particular application. It was more basic: could these tools become useful within the everyday work of an architectural office, and if so, where would that usefulness actually appear?

Like many architects, I first encountered Large Language Models through ordinary tasks. They were useful for drafting project descriptions, structuring ideas, reviewing texts, summarising information, and researching unfamiliar subjects. These activities were not spectacular, but their usefulness was immediate. They suggested that AI might be valuable not only in moments of design generation, but also in the many smaller acts of organisation, interpretation, and communication that surround architectural work.

At the same time, the open-source AI ecosystem was developing rapidly. Capable language models could be downloaded, tested, and run locally on a machine within an office. Until recently, this kind of technology had appeared to belong almost exclusively to large technology companies with significant infrastructure. The possibility of installing and evaluating these systems independently changed the character of the question.

AI was no longer something that could only be accessed through commercial services. It could be tested directly, modified, combined with other tools, and placed within the specific environment of a practice.

A powerful workstation was available in the office, and the first experiments began with local model hosting. Ollama was installed to run different Large Language Models, and OpenWebUI was introduced shortly afterwards as a more accessible conversational interface through which those models could be compared and used.

At this stage, there was no complete vision of what BlackBox would become. The first experiments simply tested what was possible. Could local models run reliably? Could different models be exchanged? Could an office interact with AI through a shared interface rather than through individual accounts and isolated tools?

These questions soon led to others:

- Could an AI system access office documentation?
- Could visual references become searchable through natural language?
- Could different specialised applications be connected into a larger environment?
- Could AI help maintain the knowledge required to use and develop the system itself?

Gradually, the technical experiments began to reveal a broader question about organisational memory and architectural knowledge. The problem was not only how to use AI, but how to connect AI to the material through which a practice already thinks and works.

AI also became part of the method through which BlackBox was developed.

Throughout the project, ChatGPT functioned as a planning and discussion partner. It helped clarify unfamiliar technical concepts, compare possible approaches, discuss software architecture, and translate broad architectural intentions into more concrete development tasks. This conversational planning became important because many of the issues being explored sat between architectural practice and software development.

As the project progressed, Codex introduced a different relationship between architectural ideas and implementation. Instead of requiring every technical challenge to be solved through conventional programming knowledge, it became possible to describe a problem, evaluate proposed solutions, test the result, and refine the implementation through an iterative dialogue with an AI system.

This revealed another important aspect of the project. Many digital challenges within architectural offices are highly specific. A practice may want to search previous competition narratives, record successful AI prompting strategies, organise visual references, document workflows, or connect internal standards with everyday work. These problems are meaningful, but they are often too particular to justify dedicated commercial software.

AI-assisted development changed the threshold for investigating them. It became possible for an architect with a clear understanding of a professional problem to begin prototyping a response without first assembling a conventional software development team.

Looking back, BlackBox was therefore not the implementation of a predetermined strategy. It was a process of discovery in which the central question became gradually clearer:

**How can artificial intelligence help an architectural practice engage with its own accumulated knowledge?**
