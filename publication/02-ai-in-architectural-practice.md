# AI in Architectural Practice

The emergence of artificial intelligence has introduced a new set of tools into architectural practice. However, the relationship between AI and architecture is still developing, and many possible directions remain open.

The most visible transformation has been AI image generation. The ability to quickly produce atmospheric visuals, references, and conceptual studies has introduced new methods of exploration and communication into the design process.

Large Language Models have also become part of everyday workflows. Architects increasingly use them for writing, summarising information, researching topics, organising thoughts, and supporting early-stage ideas.

Despite this adoption, the use of AI within many offices remains fragmented. Individuals develop their own methods, successful prompts remain personal, and useful workflows are rarely documented or shared systematically.

This reflects a broader characteristic of architectural practice.

An architectural office is not only a place where buildings are designed. It is also a repository of accumulated experience. Over time, a practice develops its own language, references, technical approaches, graphic methods, and ways of communicating ideas.

General AI systems do not possess this knowledge.

A language model may understand architecture broadly. It may know architectural history, construction principles, and publicly available information about many practices. However, it does not understand the internal culture of a particular office.

A large practice such as Zaha Hadid Architects or Foster + Partners has developed a specific way of describing projects, selecting references, structuring presentations, and approaching design questions. Much of this knowledge exists internally and has been built through decades of projects and discussions.

This raises an important possibility.

Rather than trying to create a specialised AI model from the beginning, a general AI system can be provided with the context of a specific practice through access to its organisational memory.

Previous projects, competition texts, technical standards, image libraries, details, material research, and successful AI workflows can become the contextual layer through which the AI engages with a new task.

The purpose is not to reproduce a predefined style or replace architectural judgement. The purpose is to allow an AI assistant to understand the accumulated knowledge and culture of the environment in which it operates.

This perspective shifts the discussion around AI in architecture.

The question is not only whether AI can generate new designs. A more immediate question is whether AI can help architects work more effectively with everything that already exists: previous experience, established knowledge, and the collective memory of the practice.

BlackBox emerged as a practical investigation of this possibility.

It explored whether AI could become a new interface between architects and the knowledge embedded within an architectural organisation.
