# Reflection

## Understanding where the experiment ended and what remained

By May 2026, BlackBox had reached a point where the original questions of the project had largely been explored. A local AI environment had been established, documentation systems and image archives had been connected, and conversational access to different forms of office knowledge had been tested in practice.

At the same time, it had become clear that BlackBox would not arrive at a conventional endpoint.

Every successful integration suggested additional possibilities. The image archive raised further questions about architectural metadata and semantic search. The documentation system raised questions about self-documentation and the continuity of office knowledge. The development workflow suggested a new relationship between architects and software creation. Each component began to develop its own trajectory.

This changed the status of the project. BlackBox became less a single product than an environment within which several connected investigations could take place.

That helps explain why the project never felt finished. The objective was not to deliver a definitive software platform, but to use the platform as a way of testing questions. Some experiments proved useful, others revealed limitations, and many pointed towards work that would have required a much larger and more sustained programme of development.

The practical tools used in BlackBox will inevitably change. Models will improve, interfaces will evolve, and some components of the prototype may eventually be replaced entirely. The implementation should therefore not be treated as fixed.

The questions it exposed are more durable.

Architectural practices will continue to accumulate knowledge. They will continue to inherit archives, references, workflows, project histories, standards, and lessons created through previous work. The challenge of making that knowledge accessible, useful, and transferable is unlikely to disappear.

In this respect, BlackBox may be understood less as a software experiment than as a case study in the relationship between artificial intelligence, organisational learning, and architectural practice. The prototype mattered because it made that relationship tangible. It turned an abstract discussion about AI into a set of practical encounters with documentation, images, workflows, maintenance, and development.

BlackBox did not produce a final answer to the question that initiated it. It produced a clearer understanding of the problem and a more precise set of directions for future work.
